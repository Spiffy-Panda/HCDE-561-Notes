# Course Outline

**Format:** Weekly sessions, 6:00-9:50 PM (9 weeks, adjusted for Memorial Day)   
**Cohort size:** \~37 students   
**Core Framework:** Five AI capabilities examined through strategic lenses, each carrying both constructive potential and destructive risk ("good cop / bad cop"). The course bridges individual strategic fluency with organizational strategic application.

---

## Course Architecture

The course is organized in three movements:

**Movement 1 — Orientation (Week 1):** Level-set on AI fluency, introduce the five-capability framework and the strategic lens, surface where students are in their own AI practice.

**Movement 2 — Five Capabilities (Weeks 2–4):** Deep dives into five AI capabilities, roughly two per week, with synthesis time. Each capability is examined for its constructive and destructive strategic implications. Emphasis on building critical vocabulary and analytical frameworks through the lens of adoption, reflection, and deliberate practice.

**Movement 3 — Studio Practice (Weeks 5–9):** Students shift from analysis to building. The class builds something together first, then each student develops two use cases where they incorporate AI into their own professional practice. The arc moves from shared experience to individual experimentation to stress-testing to organizational strategy to making the case. Week 9 is presentations, cohort synthesis, and close.

---

## The Five Capabilities Framework

Each capability is examined through a dual lens — what it enables and what it threatens.

### 1\. Extraction — AI Pulls Knowledge Out of People

| Constructive View | Destructive View |
| :---- | :---- |
| Makes tacit knowledge explicit and refineable | Extracts value from people, devaluing expertise |
| Enables collaboration around shared artifacts | Commoditizes specialized knowledge |
| Creates institutional memory and knowledge bases | Strips context from nuanced understanding |
| Allows rapid onboarding and knowledge transfer | Shifts power from knowledge-holders to system-owners |

**Strategic question:** When AI extracts and formalizes knowledge, who benefits from that formalization — and who loses leverage?

### 2\. Workflow Adaptation — AI Reshapes How Work Gets Done

| Constructive View | Destructive View |
| :---- | :---- |
| Augments human capability, handles routine work | Displaces jobs, hollows out roles |
| Frees people for higher-order thinking | Creates dependency on AI-mediated workflows |
| Enables new forms of work and new roles | Deskills workers over time |
| Allows scaling without proportional headcount | Concentrates gains with employers, not workers |

**Strategic question:** When AI changes a workflow, does it make the human more capable — or more replaceable?

### 3\. Compliance and Safety — AI Intersects with Risk and Responsibility

| Constructive View | Destructive View |
| :---- | :---- |
| Forces explicit articulation of quality standards | Introduces novel failure modes (hallucination, drift) |
| Enables consistent application of rules at scale | Creates false confidence in automated safety |
| AI quality controls may exceed human consistency | Obscures accountability when things go wrong |
| Makes audit trails more systematic | Regulatory landscape is chaotic and shifting |

**Strategic question:** Do AI safety controls actually make systems safer than their human-operated predecessors — or do they just make failures harder to detect?

### 4\. Model Operations — Governing and Managing AI Technologies

| Constructive View | Destructive View |
| :---- | :---- |
| Builds organizational skill in technology decisions | Creates confusion, haze, decision paralysis |
| Enables smart cost, capability, and risk tradeoffs | Vendor lock-in, capability mismatch, cost overruns |
| Develops new management disciplines (MLOps, LLMOps) | Requires expertise organizations don't yet have |
| Creates frameworks for evaluating rapidly-changing tech | Moving target — today's best practice is tomorrow's legacy |

**Strategic question:** How do organizations build the judgment to make smart AI technology decisions when the landscape shifts every quarter?

### 5\. Functional Replacement — AI as Vertical Integration

| Constructive View | Destructive View |
| :---- | :---- |
| Enables organizations to do more with less | Eliminates entire functional roles and teams |
| Reduces dependency on external vendors/specialists | Creates brittle systems that lack human resilience |
| Allows rapid capability building in new areas | Concentrates risk — failure cascades across functions |
| Potential for entirely new organizational forms | Loss of expertise makes reversal nearly impossible |

**Strategic question:** When AI replaces an entire function rather than augmenting a task, what does the organization gain — and what capacity to recover does it lose?

## Two Cross-Cutting Lenses

These lenses apply across all five capabilities and thread through the entire course:

### Lens A: Adoption as Fluency-Building

Deliberate AI adoption — paired with reflection and intentional practice — builds genuine fluency across all five capability areas. The course treats adoption not as passive acceptance but as an active, critical practice. Students develop personal adoption strategies for each capability.

### Lens B: Individual → Organizational Strategic Implications

Each capability has implications at the individual level (how it changes my work) and the organizational level (how it changes strategy, structure, and competitive position). This transition from individual to organizational thinking is where many professionals get stuck. The course explicitly scaffolds this bridge.

### Week 1: "You're Already In It"

**Theme:** Where are we, where are we going, and what does strategic AI thinking look like?

The evening arc:

1. **Welcome, introductions, three-C free-write** (20 min). Students write before they talk. Curiosity, connections, creating value. Round-robin intros: name and one sentence about your relationship with AI.  
     
2. **AI Capability Probe** (25 min). The diagnostic exercise. Students use AI live in the room to produce a short written synthesis. Instructors observe who dives in, who hesitates, who fights with it, who uses it for something unexpected. This surfaces actual fluency, not self-reported fluency.  
     
3. **Share-out: findings AND process** (20 min). Small groups share what they wrote and how they used AI to write it. Full-class debrief surfaces the range, the invisible decisions, and the meta question: "If your entire team did this every day, what would change?"  
     
4. **Video 1: Jenny Wen, "The Design Process Is Dead"** (35 min). Full 25-minute talk from Config 2025\. Wen (design lead at Anthropic, former Figma director) argues the standard design process is dead and designers need to trust intuition, start from solutions, and skip steps. Aimed directly at this cohort's training. Table reaction and share-out.  
     
5. **Course overview and five-capability framework** (20 min). The framework is positioned as part of the answer to Wen's provocation: if the old process is dead, strategic thinking about AI capabilities is what replaces blind process-following. Each capability is connected back to the probe experience and Wen's argument. Strategy vs. features vs. ethics distinction introduced.

Harris shifts the register from practitioner empowerment to systemic responsibility. The critical frameworks bridge follows: "Wen says trust yourself. Harris says the systems are bigger than you. 7. **First artifact creation** (25 min). Students create their first weekly artifact in response to the tension between Wen and Harris. Any format. This introduces the artifact practice that runs all nine weeks.  
     
8. **Gallery walk, logistics, close** (30 min). Brief gallery of artifacts, journal setup, syllabus overview, preview of Week 2\.

### Week 2: "The Knowing and the Doing"

**Theme:** How AI changes what we know and how we work

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — Extraction: The Knowledge Problem (60 min)**

The session opens with the first deep capability dive. Extraction is an ideal starting point because every student has experienced it — any time they've used a chatbot to draft, summarize, or analyze, they've participated in AI's extractive dynamic.

*Framing (15 min):* Present the extraction capability in full, drawing on examples across domains: knowledge management systems that capture expert reasoning, AI coding assistants that absorb and redistribute programming patterns, AI writing tools that flatten distinctive voice into median prose. The core tension is introduced: extraction makes knowledge explicit and shareable, but it also commoditizes and decontextualizes that knowledge.

**Key Theory: Michael Polanyi, Tacit Knowledge.** Polanyi's famous paradox, "we know more than we can tell," argues that much of human expertise lives in knowledge that can't be articulated or codified. A surgeon's feel for tissue, a designer's instinct for what "works," an experienced manager's read of a room. AI's extractive promise is that it can capture patterns from expert behavior and redistribute them. Polanyi would say the most valuable knowledge resists exactly this kind of capture. The tension between AI's extractive power and the limits Polanyi identified is the theoretical core of this capability. (*Reading: The Tacit Dimension, Chapter 1, \~20 pages; or the [infed.org overview](https://infed.org/dir/welcome/michael-polanyi-and-tacit-knowledge/) for a shorter introduction.*)

*Case Analysis (30 min):* Small groups analyze a provided case involving AI-driven knowledge extraction in a professional context (e.g., a consulting firm using AI to capture and redistribute partner expertise, or a healthcare system using AI to codify clinical judgment). Groups address:

- What knowledge is being extracted? From whom?  
- What's gained by making this knowledge explicit?  
- What's lost in the extraction — context, nuance, judgment?  
- Who benefits from the extraction? Who loses leverage?  
- What are the strategic implications for the organization? For the knowledge-holders?

*Discussion (15 min):* Surface the individual-to-organizational bridge: extraction changes individual work (my expertise is now in a system), but it also changes organizational strategy (we're less dependent on specific people, but we may be more dependent on the AI vendor).

**Hour 2 — Workflow Adaptation: The Augmentation/Displacement Spectrum (60 min)**

*Framing (15 min):* Introduce the workflow capability as a spectrum from augmentation (AI helps me do my job better) to displacement (AI does my job). Most real cases fall somewhere in between, and the strategic challenge is understanding where on that spectrum a given application lands, and where it's heading.

**Key Theory: Pfeffer & Sutton, The Knowing-Doing Gap.** Organizations consistently fail to act on what they already know. Pfeffer and Sutton documented how companies pour resources into acquiring knowledge (consultants, training, research) but can't convert that knowledge into changed behavior. The "smart talk trap" is central: talking about what to do substitutes for actually doing it. Applied to AI and workflow: most organizations know their workflows are changing. Many have studied what's coming. Very few have actually restructured how work gets done. The gap between "we know AI will change this" and "we've actually changed this" is where the strategic risk lives. AI doesn't just change workflows technically; it exposes how bad organizations already are at translating insight into action. (*Reading: [Stanford GSB summary](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/insights/knowing-doing-gap) for a quick overview; or the book's Introduction and Chapter 1, \~30 pages.*)

*Workflow Mapping Exercise (30 min):* Students map a workflow they personally know well (from their own professional experience). For each step in the workflow, they assess:

- Could AI augment this step? How?  
- Could AI replace this step? What would be lost?  
- Where on the augmentation-displacement spectrum does this fall?  
- What happens to the overall workflow quality if AI handles this step?  
- What new skills would be needed? What skills become obsolete?

*Synthesis Discussion (15 min):* Groups share patterns. Draw out the insight that workflow adaptation is never neutral — it always redistributes skill requirements, quality standards, and power. Introduce the concept of "deskilling by augmentation" — when AI handles enough of a task that humans lose the ability to do it without AI.

**Hour 3 — Cross-Capability Workshop: Extraction \+ Workflow Together (60 min)**

*Combined Case Study (45 min):* Present a richer case that involves both extraction and workflow adaptation simultaneously (e.g., a design team adopting AI tools that both capture design rationale and automate design production). Groups analyze:

- How do extraction and workflow adaptation interact in this case?  
- Does extraction enable workflow adaptation, or does workflow adaptation drive extraction?  
- What are the compounding strategic implications when both capabilities are active?  
- What would a strategically-grounded adoption plan look like that addresses both?

*Individual Reflection (15 min):* Students begin identifying potential use cases from their own practice that involve extraction or workflow adaptation. This is early-stage thinking for the Week 5-8 project.

**Hour 4 — Adoption as Practice: Building AI Fluency Deliberately (60 min)**

*Lens A Deep Dive (20 min):* Unpack the adoption-as-fluency lens. Key idea: using AI is not the same as understanding AI strategically. Adoption without reflection builds dependency; adoption with reflection builds fluency. The course advocates for a practice discipline:

- Use it (adopt deliberately)  
- Reflect on it (what happened, what worked, what was lost)  
- Analyze it (what are the strategic implications)  
- Decide about it (is this adoption worth continuing, modifying, or stopping)

*Fluency Practice Design (25 min):* Students design a personal "AI fluency practice" for the coming week — a specific adoption experiment in their own work focused on either extraction or workflow adaptation. They define:

- What they'll try  
- What they'll observe  
- What strategic questions they'll hold while experimenting  
- How they'll reflect on it

*Logistics and Preview (15 min):* Preview Week 3 topics (Compliance/Safety and Model Operations). Brief discussion of readings or preparation for next week.

**Week 2 Deliverable:** Journal entry 2 \+ Artifact 2 (from readings) \+ Learned Along

### Week 3: "Safe Enough and Smart Enough"

**Theme:** How AI intersects with risk and how we govern it

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — Compliance and Safety: The Quality Control Paradox (60 min)**

*Framing (15 min):* The compliance/safety capability presents a genuine paradox: AI systems can apply rules more consistently than humans, catch errors humans miss, and maintain audit trails at scale. But they also hallucinate, drift, fail silently, and obscure accountability. The strategic question isn't "is AI safe?" but "what does safety mean when the failure modes are fundamentally different from human failure modes?"

**Key Theory: Perrow's Normal Accidents \+ Vaughan's Normalization of Deviance.** Charles Perrow argued in *Normal Accidents* (1984) that in complex, tightly coupled systems, accidents are inevitable regardless of safety measures. Adding more safeguards actually increases complexity, which creates new categories of failure. AI safety systems are themselves complex systems layered on top of other complex systems; Perrow would predict that the guardrails will produce their own unexpected failures. Diane Vaughan's *The Challenger Launch Decision* complements Perrow with a different mechanism: the "normalization of deviance," where organizations gradually accept small deviations from correct practice because nothing bad has happened yet. Applied to AI: teams start accepting slightly wrong outputs, lower their quality bar incrementally, and drift toward dependence without noticing. Perrow explains why AI systems will fail. Vaughan explains why organizations won't notice until it's too late. (*Reading: Vaughan, "The Normalization of Deviance," [SAGE PDF](https://study.sagepub.com/system/files/Vaughan,_Diane_-_The_Normalization_of_Deviance.pdf), free, \~10 pages. Perrow referenced in lecture.*)

*Scenario Analysis (30 min):* Groups analyze parallel scenarios: a human-operated quality process and an AI-augmented version of the same process. For each:

- What failure modes exist?  
- How detectable are failures?  
- Who is accountable when things go wrong?  
- What does the audit trail look like?  
- Which system would you trust more — and why?

*Discussion (15 min):* Surface the insight that AI doesn't eliminate risk — it transforms risk. The strategic implication is that organizations need new risk frameworks, not just AI versions of existing ones. Introduce the regulatory landscape at a high level.

**Hour 2 — Model Operations: Navigating the Haze (60 min)**

*Framing (15 min):* Model operations is the least glamorous but most practically consequential capability area. It encompasses the decisions organizations make about which models to use, how to evaluate them, how to manage costs, how to handle vendor relationships, and how to build internal capability. The constructive side is genuine organizational skill-building. The destructive side is confusion, bad vendor decisions, wasted investment, and capability mismatch.

**Key Theory: Herbert Simon, Bounded Rationality.** Simon won the Nobel Prize for demonstrating that humans don't optimize; they "satisfice," choosing the first option that's good enough because they lack the cognitive capacity, information, and time to evaluate all alternatives. Organizations build routines, procedures, and decision hierarchies specifically to manage this limitation. Applied to model operations: organizations are making high-stakes AI technology decisions (which model, which vendor, build vs. buy, how much to invest) in a landscape that shifts faster than their decision-making processes can adapt. Simon would predict exactly what we see: hype-driven adoption, vendor capture, capability mismatch, and decision paralysis. The organizations that do model operations well are building new satisficing routines for a domain where the old routines don't work yet. (*Reading: [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Bounded Rationality"](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bounded-rationality/), free; or Administrative Behavior, Chapter 1, \~25 pages.*)

*Decision Framework Workshop (30 min):* Present a realistic organizational AI decision (e.g., build vs. buy for a specific capability, choosing between model providers, deciding how to allocate AI budget across functions). Groups work through a structured decision framework:

- What capability are we actually trying to build?  
- What are the cost structures (not just licensing, but integration, maintenance, training)?  
- What lock-in risks exist?  
- What internal expertise is required to manage this effectively?  
- What happens when the technology landscape shifts (which it will)?

*Discussion (15 min):* Draw out the organizational skill gap: most organizations lack the expertise to make these decisions well, and the vendors have strong incentives to sell rather than to advise. The strategic implication is that model operations capability is itself a competitive advantage.

**Hour 3 — Cross-Capability Workshop: Safety \+ Operations Together (60 min)**

*Combined Case (45 min):* Case involving both compliance/safety and model operations (e.g., a healthcare organization deciding how to deploy AI for clinical decision support — involving both patient safety considerations and technology governance decisions). Groups analyze the interplay and compounding effects.

*Reflection on Weeks 2-3 (15 min):* Students have now encountered four of five capabilities. Brief reflection: which capability feels most relevant to their own work? Which feels most challenging to think about strategically?

**Hour 4 — Fluency Check-In and Organizational Lens Introduction (60 min)**

*Fluency Practice Debrief (25 min):* Students share results from their Week 2 adoption experiment. What did they try? What did they observe? What surprised them? Small group discussion, then selected shares with full class.

*Lens B Introduction: Individual → Organizational (20 min):* Formal introduction of the organizational lens. Key insight: each capability has implications that compound at organizational scale. Extraction that affects one person's work is interesting; extraction that affects an entire function is strategic. Workflow adaptation for one task is augmentation; workflow adaptation across an enterprise is transformation. The course will increasingly push students to think at the organizational scale.

*Preview and Preparation (15 min):* Preview Week 4 (Capability 5 \+ full framework synthesis). Students should come prepared with initial thoughts on their two use cases for the project.

**Week 3 Deliverable:** Journal entry 3 \+ Artifact 3 (from readings) \+ Learned Along

### Week 4: "The Last Human in the Loop"

**Theme:** Vertical integration, full framework assembly, and the bridge to practice

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — Functional Replacement: The Vertical Integration Problem (60 min)**

*Framing (20 min):* Functional replacement is the most consequential and most contested capability. Unlike extraction or workflow adaptation, which modify existing work, functional replacement eliminates it entirely. AI doesn't help the legal team review contracts faster; it reviews contracts instead of the legal team. The vertical integration framing is key: organizations that replace functions with AI are essentially vertically integrating capabilities that were previously performed by specialized humans or external providers.

**Key Theory: Edwin Hutchins, Distributed Cognition.** Hutchins demonstrated that cognition doesn't live inside individual heads. It's distributed across people, tools, environments, and time. His landmark study of naval navigation showed that the team's intelligence emerged from the coordination between people, charts, instruments, and procedures. No single person held the full picture. When you replace a human function with AI, you don't just lose individual expertise. You lose a cognitive system that emerged from the interaction between people, and that system can't be reconstructed simply by rehiring. This is why functional replacement is often irreversible in practice: you're not just eliminating headcount, you're dismantling a form of distributed intelligence that took years to develop and that nobody fully understood while it was operating. (*Reading: Hutchins, "Distributed Cognition," [overview chapter PDF](https://arl.human.cornell.edu/linked%20docs/Hutchins_Distributed_Cognition.pdf), free, \~15 pages.*)

*Case Analysis (25 min):* Groups analyze a case of functional replacement (e.g., AI replacing a customer service function, or AI replacing a market research function). Analysis focuses on:

- What capability was replaced?  
- What was gained (speed, cost, scale)?  
- What was lost (expertise, judgment, resilience)?  
- What happens when the AI fails in a domain where human expertise has been eliminated?  
- Is this replacement reversible? At what cost?

*Discussion (15 min):* The strategic crux of functional replacement: it's often the highest-ROI AI application in the short term and the highest-risk in the long term. Organizations that replace functions lose the ability to evaluate whether the replacement is working — because the humans who could evaluate it are gone.

**Hour 2 — Full Framework Synthesis: Five Capabilities as a Strategic Analysis Tool (60 min)**

*Framework Assembly (20 min):* Walk through the full five-capability framework as an integrated analytical tool. Each capability doesn't operate in isolation — real AI deployments typically involve multiple capabilities simultaneously. The framework is presented as a diagnostic: for any AI initiative, assess which capabilities are in play, what the strategic tensions are for each, and how they compound.

*Full-Framework Exercise (25 min):* Groups apply the complete five-capability framework to a new, complex case (e.g., a company deploying AI across its entire product development process). For each capability, they identify:

- Is this capability active in this case?  
- What's the constructive strategic potential?  
- What's the destructive strategic risk?  
- What are the individual-level implications?  
- What are the organizational-level implications?

*Synthesis Discussion (15 min):* Full class discussion on how the five capabilities interact and compound. Key takeaway: strategic AI thinking requires holding all five capability dimensions simultaneously, not just optimizing for one.

**Hour 3 — The Bridge: From Individual to Organizational (60 min)**

*Lens B Deep Dive (20 min):* Formal session on the individual-to-organizational bridge. Case examples of where organizations got stuck because they thought about AI at the individual level (this tool helps my team) without considering organizational implications (this tool changes our competitive position, our cost structure, our talent requirements, our risk profile).

*Bridging Exercise (25 min):* Students take one of the capabilities they've been thinking about from their own experience and systematically "bridge" from individual to organizational implications. Structured template:

- Individual impact: How does this change my/my team's work?  
- Departmental impact: How does this change my function's role?  
- Organizational impact: How does this change the organization's strategy?  
- Industry impact: How does this change competitive dynamics?

*Pair Sharing (15 min):* Students share their bridging analysis with a partner and pressure-test each other's organizational-level thinking.

**Hour 4 — Looking Ahead: From Frameworks to Practice (60 min)**

*The Studio Phase Preview (20 min):* Introduce the studio phase (Weeks 5-9). The five-capability framework is now a tool students own. The remaining weeks are about using it — first as a class, then individually, then organizationally. The arc: build together, build for yourself, break what you built, figure out what it means for your organization, and make the case.

*Individual Reflection: Where Is AI in My Work? (25 min):* Students write a short reflection inventorying where AI currently touches (or could touch) their professional practice. Not use-case proposals yet — just honest mapping. Where am I already using it? Where am I avoiding it? Where am I curious? Where am I nervous? This inventory becomes raw material for the brainstorming in Week 5\.

*Close and Preparation (15 min):* Preview Week 5's class build exercise. Students should come ready to work — laptops charged, AI tools accessible, willingness to experiment in front of peers.

**Week 4 Deliverable:** Journal entry 4 \+ Artifact 4 (from readings) \+ AI practice inventory \+ Learned Along

### Week 5: "Get Your Hands Dirty"

**Theme:** Build something together as a class, then brainstorm where AI meets your own practice

The studio phase opens with a shared experience. Before students scatter into individual use cases, the whole class builds something together using AI — a common reference point that grounds the five-capability framework in lived, collective experience. Then students turn inward to brainstorm where AI intersects with their own professional practice.

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — The Class Build: Setup and Go (60 min)**

*Framing (10 min):* Introduce a shared design challenge — a real task that the class will accomplish together using AI tools over the next two hours. The task should be substantive enough to be interesting and involve multiple capability areas. Examples: produce a complete stakeholder briefing on a complex topic; design and prototype a service workflow; build a research synthesis from a set of provided sources. The specific task matters less than two qualities: it must be real work (not a toy exercise), and it must require the kind of judgment calls that surface strategic tensions.

Name the deal: we're going to use AI aggressively for this task. We're going to move fast. And we're going to pay very close attention to what happens — what the AI gives us, what it costs us, where we intervene, and where we don't.

*Build Sprint, Part 1 (50 min):* Break the class into 4-5 subteams of 7-8, each tackling a component of the shared challenge using AI tools of their choice. With 37 students, a single-group build would be chaos without structure. Subteams give everyone a role while still producing a shared artifact. Circulate across subteams as a collaborator but also narrate strategically in real time — pointing out capability dynamics as they happen:

- "Notice how we just extracted three people's expertise into a single document. What happened to the disagreements between them?"  
- "We just automated the formatting and structure. Did anyone check whether the AI's organizational logic actually matches what the audience needs?"  
- "That was fast. What did speed cost us?"

The room should feel energized and slightly chaotic — this is what real AI-augmented work feels like.

**Hour 2 — The Class Build: Finish and Reflect (60 min)**

*Build Sprint, Part 2 (30 min):* Complete the build. Push toward a finished artifact. The pressure of completion reveals capability tensions that leisure doesn't — when you need to ship something, which AI outputs do you trust? Where do you stop checking? What corners get cut?

*Structured Reflection: What Just Happened? (30 min):* Bring all subteams back together. The class steps back from the work product and examines the process. This is the most important 30 minutes of the session. Each subteam posts one key observation to a shared board before discussion begins, so all 4-5 perspectives are visible. Then facilitate a discussion organized around the five capabilities:

- **Extraction:** Whose knowledge went into this? What got flattened, lost, or decontextualized? What was made explicit that might have stayed tacit otherwise?  
- **Workflow:** How did the AI change the sequence of work? Where did it speed things up? Where did it create bottlenecks or rework? Did anyone feel deskilled?  
- **Compliance/Safety:** How confident are we in the accuracy of what we produced? Who checked? What's our quality standard, and did AI meet it or did we lower it?  
- **Model Operations:** We chose these tools on the fly. Was that a good decision? What would a deliberate technology choice have looked like?  
- **Functional Replacement:** Did any part of the task feel like the AI did the work *instead of* someone, rather than *helping* someone? What was that like?

This reflection should be honest and specific — grounded in things that actually happened in the room, not theoretical concerns.

**Hour 3 — Use Case Brainstorming: Where Does AI Meet Your Practice? (60 min)**

The shared build has calibrated the room. Everyone now has a common experience of AI's capabilities and tensions. Students turn to their own professional contexts.

*Individual Brainstorming (25 min):* Drawing on the personal AI practice inventory from Week 4 and the class build experience, students brainstorm as broadly as possible. The prompt: think about your professional work — past, present, and aspirational. Where could AI be incorporated? Don't filter yet. Generate volume. Think across all five capabilities:

- Where could AI extract and formalize knowledge in your domain?  
- Where could AI augment or transform workflows you know?  
- Where does AI intersect with compliance, quality, or safety in your field?  
- Where are there technology governance decisions to be made?  
- Where is functional replacement a real possibility — or already happening?

Target: 8-12 candidate ideas per student.

*Peer Expansion (20 min):* Students pair up and share their lists. The partner's job is to add ideas the student didn't think of — especially ideas in capability areas the student tends to overlook. "You've got five workflow ideas but nothing about extraction — what about the way your team documents design decisions?"

*Initial Clustering (15 min):* Students begin grouping and evaluating their ideas. Not narrowing yet — just organizing. Which ideas are strategically rich (multiple capabilities, genuine tensions)? Which are personally compelling? Which would generate the most learning?

**Hour 4 — From Ideas to Experiments (60 min)**

*Use Case Selection Criteria (15 min):* Introduce the criteria for the two use cases students will develop over the coming weeks. Each use case should be:

- **Real:** grounded in actual professional practice, not hypothetical  
- **Rich:** involves multiple capabilities and genuine strategic tensions  
- **Buildable:** something the student can actually experiment with, not just analyze  
- **Different:** the two use cases should cover different capabilities or tensions to maximize learning

The framing "Making My Life Easy" is introduced: next week, students will select their two ideas and start building. The first question is always pragmatic — can AI actually make this better, faster, easier? Start there. The strategic analysis comes from reflecting on what happens when you try.

*Shortlisting (30 min):* Students narrow from their full brainstorm to 4-5 strong candidates. For each, they write a brief sketch: what's the task, what AI capability would be involved, what strategic tension might surface, and why do I care about this one?

*Peer Pressure-Test (15 min):* Quick partner review of shortlists. Are these interesting enough? Different enough? Buildable? Partners should push back on ideas that are too safe ("use AI to summarize emails" — fine, but strategically thin) or too abstract ("explore AI's impact on my industry" — not buildable).

**Week 5 Deliverable:** Journal entry 5 \+ Artifact 5 (from class build) \+ use case brainstorm/shortlist \+ Learned Along

### Week 6: "Making My Life Easy"

**Theme:** Narrow to two ideas and build — start with the pragmatic win

Students arrive with their shortlists. This session is about making choices and then immediately making things. The framing is deliberately pragmatic: before you can analyze AI's strategic implications, you have to experience what it's like when AI actually works for you. Start with the use case where the upside is clearest — the one where AI might genuinely make your professional life easier. Build it. Use it. Then we'll complicate things.

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — Lock In Your Two Ideas (60 min)**

*Selection Workshop (30 min):* Students make their final selections. From the 4-5 candidates, choose two. Circulate to help students who are stuck, pushing them toward selections that balance pragmatic value with strategic richness. Common traps to watch for:

- Both use cases are in the same capability area (not enough range)  
- One use case is interesting but not buildable (too abstract)  
- Both use cases are "safe" — augmentation only, no real tension (not enough strategic depth)  
- A student picks what they think you want rather than what they actually care about

*Use Case Framing (30 min):* For each use case, students write a one-paragraph framing: what is the work today, what would the AI integration look like, and what's the first thing I'm going to try? This isn't analysis — it's an experiment design. The paragraph should end with a specific, concrete action: "This week I will use \[tool\] to \[task\] and pay attention to \[tension\]."

**Hour 2 — Build Sprint: Use Case 1 (60 min)**

*Live Building (45 min):* Students work on their first use case — the one where the pragmatic value is clearest. Actually using AI tools in the room to do real work from their professional practice. The energy should echo the class build from Week 5, but now it's individual.

Circulate not as a technology advisor but as a strategic provocateur. Questions to ask while students work:

- "That's working well. What's it costing you that you can't see yet?"  
- "You just accepted that without reading it carefully. Would you do that at work? Why or why not?"  
- "What would your team say if they saw you doing this?"  
- "If this works as well as it seems to be working, what changes for your organization?"

*Paired Debrief (15 min):* Quick partner exchange: what happened? What worked? What felt off? What capability dynamics did you notice?

**Hour 3 — Build Sprint: Use Case 2 (60 min)**

*Live Building (45 min):* Same structure for the second use case. Students should notice the contrast — different capability areas feel different in practice. The second use case may be harder, more ambiguous, or more strategically interesting precisely because the pragmatic win is less obvious.

*Paired Debrief (15 min):* New partner. Same reflective structure.

**Hour 4 — First Reflections: What Does "Easy" Actually Mean? (60 min)**

*Individual Reflection (20 min):* Students write a brief reflection on the day's experience. The prompt: you set out to make your life easy. Did it work? Where did AI deliver genuine value? Where did it create new problems, new dependencies, new questions? What surprised you?

*Full-Class Discussion: The "Easy" Trap (25 min):* Lead a discussion exploring the gap between "this helps me" and "this is strategically sound." The pragmatic win is real — but it can also be seductive. AI that makes individual work easier can simultaneously extract knowledge, reshape workflows, create compliance questions, require operational decisions, and edge toward functional replacement. The point isn't that "easy" is wrong — it's that "easy" is where strategic analysis starts, not where it ends.

*Week 7 Preparation (15 min):* Students should continue using AI for both use cases during the week, but with a new prompt: deliberately look for what breaks, what degrades, what gets weird with repeated use. Keep an experiment journal. Bring the failures and surprises to Week 7\.

**Week 6 Deliverable:** Journal entry 6 \+ Artifact 6 (from use case building) \+ two use case framings \+ experiment journal \+ Learned Along

### Week 7: "Now What Breaks?"

**Theme:** Stress-test the experiments, surface failure modes, find the strategic edges

Students have been building with AI for two weeks. The initial thrill of "this works" has started to develop cracks. This is the most important session in the studio sequence — the one where strategic thinking earns its keep. Anyone can identify AI's benefits. The harder skill is identifying the costs, risks, and tensions that only become visible through sustained use.

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — Studio Check-In: What Changed? (60 min)**

*Focused Reports (30 min):* At tables, students report on what shifted between Week 6 and now. The prompt is specific: where did your understanding deepen, shift, or break? Students should be noticing things they missed when everything was new: capability interactions, subtle quality degradation, shifting trust levels, workflow dependencies they didn't anticipate, moments where they stopped checking the AI's work. Each table posts their top 2-3 findings to a shared board. Pull 3-4 highlights to the full room.

*Failure and Surprise Mapping (30 min):* Full-class exercise: collectively map failures, surprises, and "that's weird" moments across all experiments onto the five-capability framework. Build a shared artifact (whiteboard, shared doc) that shows where the cohort's experiments are generating the most strategic friction. This artifact becomes a reference point for the rest of the course.

**Hour 2 — Adversarial Studio (60 min)**

*Adversarial Pairs (45 min):* Students pair up — but this time the partner's job is to break the use case, not support it. For each use case, the adversarial partner probes:

- What happens when the AI is wrong and nobody catches it?  
- What happens when you're not the user — someone less critical, less expert, less careful?  
- What happens when the organization depends on this working and it stops working?  
- What happens when the vendor changes the model, raises the price, or gets acquired?  
- What would a regulator ask about this? What would an affected community ask?  
- Where is this extracting value from people? From whom? Do they know?

The goal is to move past "this is useful" to "this is useful *and* it creates these specific vulnerabilities." Students document the vulnerabilities their partner surfaces — these become central to their strategic analysis.

*Debrief (15 min):* Brief share-out of the most surprising or consequential vulnerabilities. Highlight patterns: are certain capability tensions showing up across many experiments? Are certain failure modes universal?

**Hour 3 — Strategic Tension Analysis (60 min)**

*Five-Capability Deep Audit (40 min):* For each of their two use cases, students conduct a full five-capability analysis grounded in their actual building experience. This is not the abstract framework exercise from Week 4 — it's the framework applied to something they've lived with. For each capability:

- Is this capability active in my use case? How?  
- What's the constructive potential I've experienced firsthand?  
- What's the destructive risk I've observed or that my adversarial partner surfaced?  
- What's the strategic tension — and how would I name it clearly?

*Cross-Case Comparison (20 min):* Students compare their two use cases. Where do the strategic tensions differ? Where do they compound? What does the comparison reveal that neither case shows alone?

**Hour 4 — Sharpening and Looking Ahead (60 min)**

*Writing: The Strategic Tensions Brief (30 min):* Students write a brief (1-2 pages) that names the key strategic tensions in their use cases — not the capabilities abstractly, but the specific tensions they've experienced. This brief should be honest, specific, and grounded in real moments from the building process. It becomes the foundation for the organizational analysis in Week 8\.

*Preview: The Organizational Turn (15 min):* Preview Week 8's focus. The question shifts from "what did I learn by building?" to "what does my organization need to understand?" Students should come to Week 8 ready to think at organizational scale — and ideally, to have had a real conversation with a colleague or stakeholder about what they've been learning. That conversation becomes raw material for Week 8\.

*Preparation (15 min):* Students identify what they still need to probe or build before moving to the organizational frame. Be available for individual consultation.

**Week 7 Deliverable:** Journal entry 7 \+ Artifact 7 (from stress-testing) \+ strategic tensions briefs (both use cases) \+ Learned Along

### Week 8: "Who Else Is in the Room?"

**Theme:** From individual experiments to organizational strategy — then start making the case

This session marks the pivot from "what I learned by doing" to "what my organization needs to understand." Students have spent three weeks building, reflecting, and stress-testing. They've earned their strategic insights through practice. Now the question changes: how do you translate individual strategic fluency into organizational strategic capacity — and how do you communicate that to people who haven't been through this course?

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hour 1 — The Organizational Bridge (60 min)**

*From My Desk to the Org Chart (20 min):* Frame it: every experiment you've run is a microcosm of an organizational decision. The question isn't just "should I use AI for this?" but "should my organization adopt AI for this function — and if so, what has to be true for that to work?" Walk through a concrete example of how an individual adoption decision scales into organizational strategy, including the second- and third-order effects that only become visible at scale.

*Organizational Scenario Building (40 min):* For their primary use case, each student builds an organizational scenario. This is where the individual-to-organizational bridge becomes concrete:

- If this adoption happened across my function/department, what changes?  
- What new roles or skills are needed? What existing roles become obsolete or undervalued?  
- What governance or oversight mechanisms would need to exist?  
- What does the failure cascade look like if this goes wrong at scale?  
- What's the honest case for doing this — and what's the honest case against?  
- Which of the five capability tensions gets worse at organizational scale? Which gets better?

Students who had real conversations with stakeholders bring those perspectives in. What did the stakeholder care about that the student hadn't considered? Where was there receptivity? Where was there resistance?

**Hour 2 — Strategy Crits (60 min)**

Borrowing from design studio tradition: students present their organizational scenarios and draft strategies to small groups of 5 for critique. With 37 students, that's 7-8 groups running in parallel.

Each presentation is 5 minutes, followed by 7 minutes of critique (12 min per student, 60 min per group of 5). The critique structure:

- **Grounding check:** Is this strategy grounded in what you actually experienced building, or has it drifted into abstraction?  
- **Capability check:** Does the five-capability analysis hold up at organizational scale? Are there dimensions you're ignoring?  
- **Organizational reality check:** Would this strategy actually land? What's missing — political awareness, resource realism, change management?  
- **Courage check:** Are you being honest about the tensions, or resolving them too neatly? Where should you be recommending *against* adoption?

**Hour 3 — Making the Case: Synthesis and Presentation Framing (60 min)**

*From Two Use Cases to One Argument (30 min):* Students work on synthesizing their two use cases into a single, integrated strategic perspective. The synthesis should answer: "Based on what I built and what I learned, here is what I now understand about AI's strategic implications in my domain — and here is what I recommend." The two use cases become evidence for a larger argument, not standalone analyses.

Guiding questions:

- What did the two use cases reveal together that neither revealed alone?  
- Which capability tensions are most consequential in your domain?  
- Where is the individual-to-organizational bridge most critical — and most fragile?  
- What's the one thing you'd want a leader in your field to understand about AI strategy?

*Presentation Framing (30 min):* Frame what a strong final presentation looks like. It is not a research report. It is not a slide deck of bullet points. It is a strategic argument grounded in personal experience, analyzed through a shared framework, and extended to organizational implications. The audience should leave understanding something true about AI's strategic implications that they didn't understand before.

Structure for the Week 9 presentation:

- Lead with the strategic insight, not the process  
- Show the evidence — the moments of surprise, failure, and tension from building  
- Make the organizational bridge vivid and specific  
- Be honest about what remains unresolved  
- End with a clear recommendation — or a clear articulation of why the recommendation is hard

**Hour 4 — Preparation Studio (60 min)**

*Open Studio (45 min):* Students work on their presentations and final written synthesis. You and peers are available for consultation. The room should feel like a working studio — focused, productive, with the energy of people finishing something they've earned through weeks of building.

*Logistics and Expectations (15 min):* Final details for Week 9 presentations. Timing, format, expectations for Q\&A.

**Week 8 Deliverable:** Journal entry 8 \+ Artifact 8 (from organizational analysis) \+ draft presentation \+ organizational strategy brief \+ Learned Along. Final written synthesis due before Week 9\.

### Week 9: "Make the Case"

**Theme:** Present what you've learned, synthesize as a cohort, commit to what comes next

This is the culmination. Students present strategic arguments grounded in real building experience and analyzed through the five-capability framework. The session then widens to cohort-level synthesis — what patterns emerge across everyone's experiments — and closes with individual commitments to ongoing strategic AI practice.

**Session Structure (4 hours):**

**Hours 1-2 — Strategic Gallery Session (120 min)**

With 37 students, formal presentations for everyone would take over 9 hours. Instead, use a structured gallery format that gives every student a chance to present, every student a chance to engage with peers' work, and keeps the energy of a studio rather than a lecture hall.

*Setup (10 min):* Each student sets up a station: a poster, a projected deck on a laptop, a printed artifact collection, or whatever format fits their work. The station should be self-explanatory enough that someone can engage with it without narration, but the student will be standing there to discuss.

Each station covers:

- The two use cases: what they built, what happened, what surprised them  
- The five-capability strategic analysis: which tensions are most consequential  
- The organizational bridge: what this means beyond individual practice  
- The recommendation: what should be adopted, what should be avoided, and what remains unresolved

*Round 1 (45 min):* Half the class (18-19 students) presents at their stations. The other half circulates, spending 5-7 minutes at each station. Visitors ask questions and leave brief written feedback (sticky notes or a shared form). Encourage visitors to push presenters: "You said the extraction risk was manageable, but what if the next person using this tool doesn't have your expertise?" "Your organizational recommendation assumes leadership buy-in. What's the underground strategy?"

*Round 2 (45 min):* Switch. The other half presents, the first half circulates.

*Quick Debrief (20 min):* Full class reconvenes. Three questions by show of hands and brief discussion: What surprised you in other people's work? What pattern did you see across multiple stations? What's the one insight from someone else's project that changed how you think about your own?

**Hour 3 — Course Synthesis (60 min)**

*Pattern Synthesis (30 min):* Facilitate a discussion pulling together themes across all presentations:

- Which capabilities generated the most strategic tension across the cohort?  
- Where did the good cop/bad cop framing prove most useful — and where did it break down?  
- What organizational patterns emerged? Are there universal barriers to strategic AI adoption?  
- Where does the individual-to-organizational bridge break down most often — and why?  
- What did building and breaking teach us that analysis alone wouldn't have?

*Framework Reflection (30 min):* Revisit the five-capability framework with fresh eyes:

- What would you add or change about the framework now that you've used it in practice?  
- What did it help you see that you wouldn't have seen otherwise?  
- What are its limitations?  
- How would you use this framework in your professional practice going forward?

**Hour 4 — Fluency Reassessment and Close (60 min)**

*Fluency Reassessment (20 min):* Students redo the AI Capability Probe from Week 1: same prompt, same tools, same 15 minutes. Then compare what they produce now to what they produced in Week 1\. The comparison should be striking, not because they're better at using AI tools, but because they can think about what they're doing strategically in ways they couldn't nine weeks ago. The process capture (what did you prompt, what did you change, how much is "yours") reveals how their relationship with AI has shifted.

*Individual Strategic Commitments (20 min):* Each student writes three strategic commitments:

1. One thing I will adopt deliberately in my practice — with eyes open to the strategic tensions  
2. One thing I will advocate for in my organization — a specific conversation, proposal, or intervention  
3. One thing I will continue to learn about — the capability area or tension where I still feel least fluent

*Course Close (20 min):* Final reflections, thank-yous, and course evaluations. Close with a synthesis of the course's central insight: strategic AI thinking requires holding both the constructive and destructive potential of every capability simultaneously, and the discipline to move from individual excitement (or fear) to organizational strategy. The building made it real. The framework made it nameable. The organizational bridge made it actionable. Now go do it.

**Week 9 Deliverable:** Journal entry 9 \+ Artifact 9 \+ final presentation \+ final reflection \+ Learned Along

## 1\. Weekly Journal (15 points)

The weekly journal combines two things: Three-C reflection framework (curiosity, connections, creation) and the Learned Along check-in. The goal is a single, lightweight weekly habit that captures what you're thinking without becoming a burden.

### What You Submit Each Week

**Part A: Three-C Reflection (the thinking part)** Spend about 25 minutes writing. Free writing is fine. It does not need to be polished or make a formal argument. Your future self just needs to be able to read it.

- **Curiosity.** What is your strategic human-centered AI mind curious about this week? What questions are forming?  
- **Connections.** What connections are you making? Between parts of the class, between class and your current work, between class and where you're headed. Anything that links.  
- **Creating Value.** How does this week's experience connect to creating value, for you or others? Are possibilities starting to take shape? Are there goals you want to set?

**Part B: Learned Along (the quick part)** Two sentences. That's it. Answer three prompts in two sentences total, or one sentence each, however it comes out:

- One thing you **liked** from this week  
- One thing you **learned** from this week  
- One thing you **longed for** (wished we'd covered, wanted more of, felt was missing)

### Format and Logistics

The journal lives wherever makes sense to you: a Google Doc, a slide deck, Figma, Miro, a physical notebook you photograph. The form doesn't matter. What matters is that you do it consistently and that you can share it with instructors.

**Submission:** Weekly via Canvas. Share a link, upload a PDF, or post a screenshot. Due by Monday at noon following each class session.

**In class:** A small portion of each session (roughly 15 minutes) will be used to surface commonalities across journal entries, building shared themes and community awareness.

### Grading

15 points across 9 weeks. Graded on completion and genuine engagement, not on quality of prose. A thoughtful, honest entry that shows you're thinking about the material gets full marks.

## 2\. Weekly Readings and Artifacts (15 points)

Every week, you make something. By the end of the course, you have a collection of things that adds up to a toolkit you take with you.

### The Idea

Each week, you distill one strategic insight about AI into a form you could share with someone who wasn't in the room. One insight. Shareable without you narrating it. The form is completely up to you. The constraint is what makes it cohere. The freedom is what makes it interesting.

Your weekly artifact could be:

- A card you'd hand to a colleague before a meeting  
- A poster you'd put on a wall  
- A one-page zine  
- A sticker  
- A diagram on a napkin  
- A 30-second video  
- A physical object  
- A letter to your future self  
- A slide you'd actually put in a deck at work  
- A provocation printed on a coffee mug  
- A mantra, a warning, a question, a rule of thumb  
- Something we haven't thought of

The only requirements: (1) it distills one insight, (2) someone else can engage with it without you explaining it, and (3) you'd actually want to keep it.

By Week 9, you'll have a collection of 8 or 9 of these. That collection is your strategic AI toolkit. It's personal. It reflects how you think about this material. Some of you will end up with a card deck. Some of you will end up with a poster series. Some of you will end up with something none of us anticipated. The collection should feel like yours, and it should be something you'd actually pull out six months from now when you're in a meeting and someone says "should we use AI for this?"

### Inspirations

Three projects that show what this kind of personal, accumulative, shareable artifact-making can look like:

[**The Tarot Cards of Tech**](https://tarotcardsoftech.artefactgroup.com/) (Artefact, Seattle). A deck of provocation cards designed to inspire conversations about the true impact of technology. Each card poses a question that reframes how you think about a product or system. The cards work in brainstorms, team meetings, or design reviews. They're compact, portable, and conversation-starting. Your artifacts could work this way: small, sharp, useful in a real professional context.

[**Things I Have Learned in My Life So Far**](https://bibliografik.com/blog/everything-ive-learned-sagmeister) (Stefan Sagmeister). A running list of personal maxims transformed into wildly varied typographic artworks: words spelled out on the backs of swimmers, displayed by inflatable monkeys, spelled in bananas, flaming on bamboo scaffolding. Each piece is a single sentence Sagmeister believes, rendered in a form that demands attention. The lesson: the constraint is one insight per piece. The form is limitless. The personal conviction is what makes it land.

[**Dear Data**](http://www.dear-data.com/theproject) (Giorgia Lupi and Stefanie Posavec). Two information designers spent a year collecting personal data on a different topic each week (complaints, laughter, phone use, doors) and hand-drawing their findings on postcards they mailed across the Atlantic. Every postcard is a unique visual system with a hand-drawn legend on the back. The originals are in MoMA's permanent collection. The lesson: a rigorous personal practice, done consistently over time, accumulates into something much larger than any single piece. Data and self-observation become beautiful, honest, and deeply human.

### How It Connects to Readings

During Weeks 1 through 4, your weekly artifact responds to assigned readings or in-class material. The readings connect you to the larger professional and critical conversations around AI. The artifact is how you process that material. Making the thing IS the thinking. Budget about an hour for reading plus artifact creation.

During the studio weeks (5-8), your artifacts shift to capturing insights from your own building and experimentation with AI. The readings get lighter. The artifacts stay personal.

### How It Works In Class

You bring your artifact to class. Small groups share them and use them as the basis for discussion. Your artifact's job is to start a conversation without you having to narrate everything.

After group discussion, we surface themes and "reachable moments" as a full class. Over the weeks, you'll see each other's artifacts accumulate. Patterns will emerge. People will make things you didn't expect. That's part of the learning.

### The Tone We're Going For

We can certainly do serious seriousness. But can we also tap into fun seriousness, beautiful seriousness, mindful seriousness? Rigorous playfulness and disciplined surprise are welcome here. The readings can be heavy. The artifacts don't have to be.

### Submission

Upload or share your artifact via Canvas before each class session. If your artifact is physical, photograph it. If it's digital, share the link or file. If it's a 30-second video, upload it. Whatever the form, make sure someone who wasn't there can engage with it.

### Grading

15 points across 9 weekly artifacts. Graded on engagement with the material, the artifact's capacity to communicate an insight, and evidence that you're building a collection that accumulates into something meaningful. There is no "right" format. Consistency and genuine effort matter more than polish.

## 3\. Project (30 points)

### Overview

You identify a use case from your professional practice (or aspirational practice) where AI could be meaningfully incorporated. Over Weeks 5 through 8, you build it, use it, stress-test it, and analyze its strategic implications through the five-capability framework. This is not a hypothetical analysis. You actually use AI to do real work, and then you reflect on what happened.

### What You Produce

**Experiment Journal Entries (ongoing, Weeks 5-7)** As you build and use your AI integration, you keep a running journal. What did you try? What worked? What broke? What surprised you? What strategic tensions surfaced? The journal should be honest and specific, grounded in actual moments from your practice.

**Five-Capability Strategic Analysis (Week 7\)** After stress-testing in Week 7's adversarial session, you produce a strategic tensions brief for this use case. For each of the five capabilities (extraction, workflow adaptation, compliance/safety, model operations, functional replacement), you document: is this capability active, what constructive potential did you experience, what destructive risk did you observe or have surfaced by peers, and what is the strategic tension in specific terms.

**Organizational Scenario (Week 8\)** You extend the analysis from individual experience to organizational scale. If this adoption happened across your function or department, what would change? What roles shift, what governance is needed, what failure cascades are possible? Include both the honest case for adoption and the honest case against.

**Final Presentation Component (Week 9\)** This use case becomes half of your final presentation. You present what you built, what happened, what you learned strategically, and what the organizational implications are.

### Week 2: "The Knowing and the Doing"

*Capabilities: Extraction \+ Workflow Adaptation* *Key theories: Polanyi (tacit knowledge) \+ Pfeffer & Sutton (knowing-doing gap)*

**Required:**

1. **Michael Polanyi, "Tacit Knowing" overview** (web, \~15 min read) Polanyi's paradox, "we know more than we can tell," is the theoretical heart of extraction. This overview covers the core concepts: tacit vs. explicit knowledge, the "from-to" structure of awareness, and why the most valuable human expertise resists codification. Read this asking: if Polanyi is right, what can AI actually extract, and what will it always miss? [infed.org](https://infed.org/dir/welcome/michael-polanyi-and-tacit-knowledge/)  
     
2. **Stanford GSB, "The Knowing-Doing Gap" summary** (web, \~10 min read) Pfeffer and Sutton's core argument: organizations consistently fail to act on what they know. The "smart talk trap" (talking about what to do substitutes for doing it) applies directly to how organizations respond to AI-driven workflow change. Read this asking: why do organizations that know AI will reshape their work still fail to actually change how work gets done? [Stanford GSB](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/insights/knowing-doing-gap)  
     
3. **Matt Beane, "How Do We Learn to Work with Intelligent Machines?" (TED, 2018\)** (\~12 min) Beane's research on how AI disrupts apprenticeship in surgery. When AI captures expert knowledge into a system, junior workers lose the pathway through which they would have developed that expertise. The concept of "shadow learning" connects Polanyi's tacit knowledge directly to the workflow adaptation question. [TED Talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/matt_beane_how_do_we_learn_to_work_with_intelligent_machines)

**Optional:**

- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond, "Generative AI at Work" (NBER Working Paper 31161, 2023). The landmark study of 5,179 customer service workers. AI improved novice productivity 34% by extracting and redistributing expert patterns. Pairs with Beane: extraction helps novices short-term but may erode the pipeline that produces experts. Free PDF. [NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161)  
- Maggie Appleton, "The Expanding Dark Forest and Generative AI" (2023). What happens when AI floods the internet with content that mimics expertise. Beautifully written. [Essay](https://maggieappleton.com/ai-dark-forest)  
- Matt Beane, "Learning to Work with Intelligent Machines" (*Harvard Business Review*, Sept-Oct 2019). The longer version of the TED argument. [HBR](https://hbr.org/2019/09/learning-to-work-with-intelligent-machines)

### Week 3: "Safe Enough and Smart Enough"

*Capabilities: Compliance/Safety \+ Model Operations* *Key theories: Perrow (normal accidents) \+ Vaughan (normalization of deviance) \+ Simon (bounded rationality)*

**Required:**

1. **Diane Vaughan, "The Normalization of Deviance"** (SAGE study guide, free PDF, \~10 pages) Vaughan documented how NASA gradually accepted small deviations from safety standards because nothing bad had happened yet, until the Challenger exploded. Applied to AI: teams start accepting slightly wrong outputs, lower their quality bar incrementally, and drift toward dependence without noticing. Read this asking: where are you already normalizing AI's deviations in your own practice? [SAGE PDF](https://study.sagepub.com/system/files/Vaughan,_Diane_-_The_Normalization_of_Deviance.pdf)  
     
2. **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Bounded Rationality"** (web, read Sections 1-3, \~20 min) Simon's core argument: humans don't optimize, they "satisfice" (pick the first good-enough option). Organizations build routines to manage this limitation. Applied to model operations: organizations are making high-stakes AI technology decisions in a landscape that shifts faster than their decision-making can adapt. Read this asking: where are you or your organization satisficing on AI decisions, and is that good enough? [Stanford Encyclopedia](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bounded-rationality/)  
     
3. **Air Canada chatbot case** (web article, \~5 min read) In 2024, a tribunal found Air Canada liable for its chatbot's fabricated bereavement fare policy. Air Canada argued the chatbot was "a separate entity." The tribunal disagreed. Concrete, brief, vivid. Connects Vaughan's normalization of deviance to a real AI compliance failure. [AI Business](https://aibusiness.com/nlp/air-canada-held-responsible-for-chatbot-s-hallucinations-) | [ABA analysis](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-law-today/2024-february/bc-tribunal-confirms-companies-remain-liable-information-provided-ai-chatbot/)

**Optional:**

- NIST, "AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)" Executive Summary (\~20 pages). The most comprehensive U.S. framework for AI risk governance. Four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. Free PDF. [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) | [PDF](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf)  
- Deloitte, "How Can Tech Leaders Manage Emerging Generative AI Risks Today?" (2024). Four categories of gen AI risk. Includes the stat that 38% of executives made incorrect decisions based on hallucinated outputs. [Deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/topics/digital-transformation/four-emerging-categories-of-gen-ai-risks.html)  
- Hard Fork podcast (NYT), selected recent episode on AI governance or safety (\~60 min). [Apple Podcasts](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/hard-fork/id1528594034)

### Week 4: "The Last Human in the Loop"

*Capability: Functional Replacement / Vertical Integration* *Key theory: Hutchins (distributed cognition)*

**Required:**

1. **Edwin Hutchins, "Distributed Cognition"** (overview chapter, free PDF, \~15 pages) Hutchins demonstrated that cognition doesn't live inside individual heads. It's distributed across people, tools, environments, and time. A naval navigation team's intelligence emerges from the coordination between people, charts, instruments, and procedures. When you replace a human function with AI, you don't just lose individual expertise. You lose a cognitive system that can't be reconstructed by rehiring. Read this asking: what distributed cognitive systems exist in your organization that nobody fully understands while they're operating? [Cornell PDF](https://arl.human.cornell.edu/linked%20docs/Hutchins_Distributed_Cognition.pdf)  
     
2. **Zillow iBuying failure** (web article, \~10 min read) Zillow's algorithm overvalued properties, leading to $500M+ losses and 25% workforce layoffs. Critically, the company adjusted algorithms to meet business targets rather than follow accurate predictions. A case study of functional replacement gone wrong: Zillow tried to replace human real estate judgment with AI, discovered the AI couldn't do the job, and the humans were already gone. [GeekWire](https://www.geekwire.com/2021/ibuying-algorithms-failed-zillow-says-business-worlds-love-affair-ai/)

**Optional:**

- F. A. Hayek, "The Use of Knowledge in Society" (*American Economic Review*, 1945). The classic argument that knowledge required for planning is dispersed across many individuals, and no central authority can aggregate it effectively. When AI replaces a function, it centralizes what was distributed. Free. [Econlib](https://www.econlib.org/library/Essays/hykKnw.html)  
- McKinsey, "The State of AI: How Organizations Are Rewiring to Capture Value" (March 2025). 78% of organizations use AI, but only one-third scale it. The gap between adoption and strategic maturity. [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value)

### Weeks 5-8: Studio Phase

During the studio phase, readings are light and practical. One required piece per week, oriented toward the work students are doing. All are web-accessible.

**Week 5: "Get Your Hands Dirty"**

- **Required:** Dan Saffer, "Notes on Donald Schon's The Reflective Practitioner" (Medium, \~10 min read). A designer-oriented summary of Schon's core ideas: reflection-in-action, knowing-in-action, the reflective conversation with materials. Grounds the studio practice in theory. [Medium](https://odannyboy.medium.com/notes-on-donald-sh%C3%B6ns-the-reflective-practitioner-e67f753879d8)  
- **Optional:** Goldsworthy & Forlano, "Advancing Donald Schon's Reflective Practitioner: Where to Next?" (*Design Issues*, MIT Press, 2023). Revisits Schon for AI-mediated design. [MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/desi/article/39/3/3/116622/Advancing-Donald-Schon-s-Reflective-Practitioner)

**Week 6: "Making My Life Easy"**

- **Required:** Ethan Mollick, "15 Times to Use AI, and 5 Not To" (One Useful Thing, 2024, \~10 min read). Practical taxonomy of when AI helps vs. when it undermines judgment. Central tension: AI is most useful where you're expert enough to catch mistakes, but least helpful for the deep work that made you expert. [One Useful Thing](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/15-times-to-use-ai-and-5-not-to)

**Week 7: "Now What Breaks?"**

- **Required:** Evidently AI, "When AI Goes Wrong: 13 Examples of AI Mistakes and Failures" (web, \~15 min read). A catalog of real-world AI failures: Air Canada, Zillow, McDonald's drive-thru AI, and more. What went wrong, what could have prevented it. Pattern recognition for stress-testing your own builds. [Evidently AI](https://www.evidentlyai.com/blog/ai-failures-examples)  
- **Optional:** Promptfoo, "OWASP Red Teaming: A Practical Guide to Getting Started" (web). Hands-on adversarial testing techniques for LLM applications. [Promptfoo](https://www.promptfoo.dev/blog/owasp-red-teaming/)

**Week 8: "Who Else Is in the Room?"**

- **Required:** HBR, "Overcoming the Organizational Barriers to AI Adoption" by Li, Zhu, and Hua (November 2025). 45% of executives found AI ROI below expectations. Real barriers: fear of replacement, rigid workflows, entrenched power. Organizations that redesign incentives and governance capture value; those that just deploy tools don't. [HBR](https://hbr.org/2025/11/overcoming-the-organizational-barriers-to-ai-adoption)  
- **Optional:** BCG, "From Potential to Profit: Closing the AI Impact Gap" (2025). The 10-20-70 principle: 10% algorithms, 20% data/tech, 70% people/process/culture. [BCG](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/closing-the-ai-impact-gap)

## Part 2: Critical Theory Reference Library

These readings are not assigned to specific weeks. They form a reference library students can draw from when building artifacts, analyzing use cases, or developing organizational scenarios. Each entry notes which of the five capabilities it connects to most directly.

Students are encouraged (not required) to engage with at least one critical theory reading during the studio phase (Weeks 5-8) that connects to their use case domain. The artifact for that week could respond to the critical reading.

---

### Anti-Racist Perspectives on AI

1. **Ruha Benjamin, *Race After Technology* (Polity, 2019), Introduction: "The New Jim Code"** (\~25 pages) \[library PDF\] Shows how technologies that appear neutral replicate or worsen racial bias. Three types of discriminatory design: technologies that amplify hierarchies, technologies that ignore and replicate divisions, and technologies that aim to fix bias but do the opposite. **Connects to:** Extraction, Functional Replacement  
     
2. **Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (NYU Press, 2018), Chapter 1** \[library PDF\] How Google's search algorithms privilege whiteness and discriminate against people of color. Challenges the neutrality of algorithmic systems. Nobel won a MacArthur Fellowship in 2021 for this work. **Connects to:** Extraction, Compliance/Safety  
     
3. *Accessible alternative:* Ruha Benjamin, TED Talk or Radical AI podcast interview (\~30-60 min)

---

### Labor and Exploitation

1. **Kate Crawford, *Atlas of AI* (Yale, 2021), Chapter 2: "Labor"** (\~30 pages) \[library PDF\] Documents the hidden human labor behind "automation": tagging, correcting, evaluating, editing AI systems. "Thousands of people are needed to support the illusion of automation." **Connects to:** Extraction, Workflow Adaptation  
     
2. **Mary L. Gray and Siddharth Suri, *Ghost Work* (Houghton Mifflin, 2019), Introduction** (\~25 pages) \[library PDF\] Coined the term "ghost work" for labor performed by humans but believed by customers to be automated. Work isn't disappearing; it's being hidden. **Connects to:** Workflow Adaptation, Functional Replacement  
     
3. *Accessible alternative:* Data & Society, Databite No. 119: Mary L. Gray on Ghost Work (\~60 min). [Data & Society](https://datasociety.net/library/databite-no-119-mary-l-gray/)

---

### Feminist and Gender Perspectives

1. **Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein, *Data Feminism* (MIT Press, 2020), Chapter 1: "The Power Chapter"** (\~30 pages, Open Access) Intersectional feminist theory applied to data science. "Examine power" as the first principle. [MIT Press (Open Access)](https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu/) **Connects to:** Extraction, Compliance/Safety  
     
2. **Os Keyes, "The Misgendering Machines" (*Proceedings of ACM HCI*, 2018\)** (\~25 pages) How automated gender recognition causes harm even when it "works" by accuracy standards. A case for questioning whether a system should exist, not just whether it performs well. [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3274357) **Connects to:** Compliance/Safety, Functional Replacement

---

### Decolonial Perspectives

1. **Mohamed, Png, and Isaac, "Decolonial AI" (*Philosophy & Technology*, 2020\)** (free PDF) AI systems replicate colonial power dynamics through data extraction, knowledge hierarchies, and governance structures that exclude marginalized voices. Three tactics: critical technical practice, reverse tutelage, renewal of political communities. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13347-020-00405-8) | [arXiv PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2007.04068) **Connects to:** Extraction, Model Operations  
     
2. *Accessible alternative:* MIT Technology Review, "The Problems AI Has Today Go Back Centuries" (2020). [MIT Tech Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/07/31/1005824/decolonial-ai-for-everyone/)

---

### Design Justice

1. **Sasha Costanza-Chock, *Design Justice* (MIT Press, 2020), Chapter 1** (\~30 pages, Open Access) Design justice is about power, not just inclusion. Challenges universalist design principles that erase intersectionally disadvantaged groups. [MIT Press (Open Access)](https://designjustice.mitpress.mit.edu/) | [Full PDF](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/43542/external_content.pdf) **Connects to:** All five capabilities  
     
2. *Accessible alternative:* Data & Society, Databite: Design Justice with Sasha Costanza-Chock (\~60 min). [Data & Society](https://datasociety.net/library/design-justice/)

---

### Surveillance and Control

1. **Virginia Eubanks, *Automating Inequality* (St. Martin's, 2018), Introduction \+ Chapter 4: "The Allegheny Algorithm"** (\~50 pages) \[library PDF\] Automated decision-making in welfare and child protective services reproduces patterns of punishing poverty. The "digital poorhouse" acts faster and more opaquely than its physical predecessors. **Connects to:** Compliance/Safety, Functional Replacement  
     
2. *Accessible alternative:* Coded Bias (documentary, 2020, \~90 min). Joy Buolamwini's investigation into facial recognition bias. Available on streaming platforms.

---

### Algorithmic Fairness and Its Limits

1. *Selbst, boyd, Friedler, Venkatasubramanian, and Vertesi, "Fairness and Abstraction in Sociotechnical Systems" (FAT '19, 2019\)*\* (free PDF, \~15 pages) Five "abstraction traps" that make technical fairness interventions ineffective: Framing, Portability, Formalism, Ripple Effect, Solutionism. Why technically "fair" AI can still cause harm. [PDF](https://sorelle.friedler.net/papers/sts_fat2019.pdf) **Connects to:** Compliance/Safety, Functional Replacement
